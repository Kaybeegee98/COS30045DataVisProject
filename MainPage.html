Inflation Rates

What is Inflation Rate and Why is it Important?

A brief description of the inflation rate is that it is simply the rate of increase in prices over a given period. Typically if a country has a higher inflation rate, it means the cost of living increases quicker than those countries with a lower inflation rate. When looking at different countries migrating to Australia, a question we wanted to answer was, “Why do people migrate to Australia?”. A generally lower cost of living was one of our proposed answers to this question, especially when combined with a lower unemployment rate (more on that later). The below graph is set up to allow users to view the difference in inflation rates between countries moving to Australia and Australia itself. From this, we hope we can see patterns in the inflation rates that might describe why people are migrating to Australia. The rationale we are thinking about during this is if a country has a disproportionately large inflation rate when compared to Australia, then the migration rate to Australia should be larger.

Inflation Rate Results

Looking at all the different inflation rates compared to each other, we should be able to determine if the Inflation rate has some sway over why people migrate to Australia. From the different charts, the countries that have the worst inflation rates over the years would appear to belong to;

  • India, who had the consistently highest inflation rate
  • England, who has had the highest inflation rate since October 2021
  • South Africa, which may not be the highest but has never been lower than Australia
When comparing this to the Overseas Born Population pie chart (which looked at overseas migration in the year 2020), we can see that the two countries with the most migration were India and England. New Zealand sits at the fourth highest migration tally, even though its inflation rate is comparable to Australia.


Unemployment Rates

What is Unemployment Rate and Why Does it Matter?

The unemployment rate is the amount of the available labour force that does not currently hold a job. In this instance, we are looking at individuals over the age of 18 for the labour force. The higher the unemployment rate is, the less people are working. This can be caused by a few different factors, including but not limited to;

  • Economic Downturn
  • Education
  • Job Availability
  • Amount of individuals entering the labour force at a given time
While many of these factors are difficult to track, one is especially important for our data, and that is an economic downturn. Due to effect, COVID-19 had on the world during the years 2020 and 2021, we can presume to see an increase in unemployment rates for all countries during this time. That is due to workplaces not earning enough money due to a lack of customers, as such needing to fire staff. For the sake of our analysis, we are looking to see if there is a correlation between Australia having a better employment rate (lower unemployment) than other countries. This should show us if a large number of people are migrating to Australia for work.

Unemployment Rate Results

After analysing the Stacked Bar Graph, we are able to figure out a few things. Firstly, South Africa has a consistent unemployment rate of around 30%, which is disproportionally higher than any other country. It would be safe to assume anyone migrating from South Africa to Australia would be doing so for work. Moving on, we can see that before 2020, three countries had, on average, a lower unemployment rate than Australia. These countries are England, China and New Zealand. After July 2021, both China's and England’s unemployment rates became more comparable to Australia’s, staying relatively close to each other. New Zealand however, continued to have a lower unemployment rate than any other country on the list.
From this, we can gather a few things:
Migrators from countries such as the Philippines, South Africa and India most likely move due to there being a much lower unemployment rate in Australia.
For the other countries on this list, while Australia’s unemployment rate is by no definition poor, we can assume that there are most likely other factors contributing to individuals seeking to migrate.


Conclusion

Looking at every piece of information gathered from the graphs above, we can begin to make some assumptions.
Firstly, both England and China have had historically higher inflation rates (with the exception of China after October 2020) while having lower unemployment rates than Australia. This means that, while those countries are theoretically easier to find jobs in, the higher inflation rate has pushed people to migrate to Australia, which has the combined bonus of both a low inflation rate and a low unemployment rate.
Secondly, countries such as South Africa, The Philippines and India have both a high inflation rate and a high unemployment rate. This combination makes it easy to see that people migrating to Australia are seeking to have a lower cost of living as well as more job opportunities available.
Lastly, the only country that seems to cause issues for our theory is New Zealand. Both it’s and Australia’s inflation rates, as well as the unemployment rates, are practically identical. The only major difference is New Zealand’s recent spike in the inflation rate, would doesn’t describe why, historically, New Zealand contains the fourth highest amount of overseas-born population that migrates to Australia.

With these assumptions in mind, we believe it is safe to assume that the Inflation Rate and Unemployment Rate are two major contributing factors as to why people migrate to Australia. With Australia holding such a large number of cultures in it, it makes it easy for these countries to migrate to Australia for the above-mentioned reasons, with still holding onto their home country’s community.
